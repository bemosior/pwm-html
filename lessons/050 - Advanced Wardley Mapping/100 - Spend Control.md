In Wardley Mapping, spend control is a policy that limits what kind of work gets funded and launched by requiring that it first be mapped. For example, any project that will cost $20,000 or more must be mapped before it can be considered.

In Simon's experience, waste is abundant in large organizations. People make decisions based on their feelings. They read an HBR article and jump on the bandwagon of the latest trends. "3D Television is the Future" (or NFTs, or whatever). Projects get launched because of ego.

Maybe there are some good ideas, too. Heck, maybe they're all good ideas. But even if they are, in all likelihood they're incoherent as a collective portfolio and incoherent in terms of what they do together as part of a strategy.

So to sum up so far, waste and nonsense and incoherence is the status quo.

Given that, Simon would like to suggest an alternative: map out the work before starting it, so it is less likely to be a complete waste of time.

After all, to make a map you are forced to consider users, needs, capabilities, relationships, and market forces (evolution). And if a proposal has been mapped, then it also is much more critiqueable by others. With critique (or as Wardley calls it, "challenge"), bad ideas can die before they get funded, or they can at least be made much better! So, spend control limits spend by halting the frictionless launch of ill-considered work.

Returning to aggregation, what Simon also suggests is having a central working group responsible for gathering and collating maps created as part of the spend control policy. That way there's at least a chance that someone will notice patterns of duplication and bias across the maps made by different parts of the organization.
